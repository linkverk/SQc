=== Slide 7 ===
A SHORT HISTORY
The first cryptographic tools can be traced back to the seventh century BC, when Spartan warriors in Ancient Greece would pass secret messages to each other using a simple method of encryption: the cipher.
- Cipher: A system of writing that prevents most people from understanding the message.
This cryptographic technique served two main purposes:
- protecting the integrity of messages
- maintaining secrecy

=== Slide 8 ===
The Caesar Cipher, devised by Julius Caesar, was another early encryption method that relied on shifting letters around so that each character in the cipher represented a letter at a different position in the alphabet.

=== Slide 9 ===
APPLICATIONS OF CRYPTOGRAPHY
1) Confidentiality:
- Only the intended or authorized recipient can understand the message.
- It can also be referred to as privacy or secrecy.

2) Data Integrity:
- Data cannot be forged or modified by an adversary intentionally or by unintended/accidental errors.
- Though data integrity cannot prevent the alteration of data, it can provide a means of detecting whether the data was modified.

=== Slide 10 ===
APPLICATIONS OF CRYPTOGRAPHY (continued)
3) Authentication:
- The authenticity of the sender is assured and verifiable by the receiver.

4) Non-repudiation:
- The sender, after sending a message, cannot deny later that they sent the message.
- This means that an entity (a person or a system) cannot refuse the ownership of a previous commitment or an action.

=== Slide 11 ===
LET'S SUMMARIZE IT:
- Any information in the form of a text message, numeric data, or a computer program can be called plaintext.
- The idea is to encrypt the plaintext using an encryption algorithm and a key that produces the ciphertext.
- The ciphertext can then be transmitted to the intended recipient, who decrypts it using the decryption algorithm and the key to get the plaintext.

=== Slide 12 ===
HOW IS DATA ENCRYPTED?
When encrypting, one typically passes a message and a key through an algorithm.
- Encryption: The algorithm converts the message into something unreadable, aided by the key.
- Decryption: To recreate the message from the unreadable stream of data, one needs an inverse function and a matching key.

=== Slide 13 ===
EXAMPLE
- The stronger the algorithm and the key, the more difficult it is for the adversary to attack.

=== Slide 14 ===
TYPES OF CRYPTOGRAPHY
There are two kinds of cryptography:
- Symmetric key (private key) cryptography
- Asymmetric key (public key) cryptography

=== Slide 15 ===
SYMMETRIC ENCRYPTION
Symmetric encryption uses the same key for both encryption and decryption.
- Symmetric encryption works well if the sending and receiving parties of a message can exchange the secret key over a channel that's unreachable by attackers.
- It works great when the sender and receiver are the same, such as when a person or a program wants to encrypt information for later retrieval.

=== Slide 16 ===
The main challenge in symmetric encryption is how to share the key.
- It is obvious that we cannot pass the key along with the cipher-text (the encrypted message).
- One may occasionally use another channel to pass the key, such as traditional mail or a phone call, but a second communication channel is not always available.

=== Slide 17 ===
LIMITATIONS OF SYMMETRIC KEY CRYPTOGRAPHY
Some limitations in symmetric key cryptography:
- The key must be shared by the sender and receiver before any communication. It requires a secured key establishment mechanism in place.
- The sender and receiver must trust each other, as they use the same symmetric key.
  - If a receiver is hacked by an attacker or the receiver deliberately shared the key with someone else, the system gets compromised.
- A large network of n nodes require n(n-1)/2 key pairs to be managed.
- It is advisable to keep changing the key for each communication session.
- Often a trusted third party is needed for effective key management, which itself is a big issue.

=== Slide 18 ===
ASYMMETRIC KEY CRYPTOGRAPHY
There are two keys in asymmetric cryptography:
- Public keys are known and accessible to everyone.
  - They can be used to encrypt the message or to verify the signatures.
- Private keys are extremely private to individuals.
  - They are used to decrypt the message or to create signatures.
The public key should be kept in a public repository accessible to everyone and the private key should be kept as a well-guarded secret.

=== Slide 19 ===
EXAMPLE
Any safe box can be locked with any key, but only the corresponding key pair can unlock it.
For example:
- If the box is locked with Mike's Private key, only Mike's Public key can open it.
- If the box is locked with Mike's Public key, only Mike's Private key can open it.

=== Slide 22 ===
DIGITAL SIGNATURE (INTRODUCTION)
Asymmetric key algorithms can be utilized to sign a message digitally.
- There are three main reasons for applying a digital signature:
  - Authentication: to validate the source of a message
  - Nonrepudiation: A sender cannot deny the transmission of a message if it is digitally signed
  - Integrity: preserves the integrity of a message by not allowing it to alter in transit

RSA ALGORITHM
RSA algorithm (Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman) is possibly one of the most widely used public-key cryptographic algorithms for encrypting and signing messages.

Confidentiality:
- Encryption/Decryption

Authentication (Digital Signature):
- Authentication

=== Slide 25 ===
HYBRID MODEL
Asymmetric encryption is generally extremely slow compared to symmetric encryption.
- Most cryptographic schemes in use, such as PGP and SSL/TLS, thus use hybrid encryption.
- The message is encrypted using a symmetric algorithm with a randomly generated key, and this one-time key is communicated using asymmetric encryption.
- Hybrid encryption even makes it possible to pass the same message to many recipients without too much overhead.
- We only need to encrypt the symmetric key rather than the entire message using the public key of all recipients.

HASH FUNCTION
A hash function is any function that can be used to map data of arbitrary size to fixed-size values.

=== Slide 29 ===
COLLISION
- If two pieces of data in a hash table share the same hash value!

=== Slide 30 ===
CRYPTOGRAPHIC HASH FUNCTION
- A cryptographic hash function is a one-way function that converts input data of arbitrary length and produces a fixed-length output.
- The output is usually termed "hash value" or "message digest".

=== Slide 31 ===
COLLISION (in cryptographic context)
[Diagram: two different inputs "fxw2j" and "br2f0" producing the same hash = Hash collision]

=== Slide 32 ===
CORE FEATURES IN CRYPTOGRAPHIC HASH FUNCTION
1. Input can be any string of any size, but the output is of fixed length, say, a 256-bit output or a 512-bit output as examples.
2. The hash value should be efficiently computable for any given message.
3. It is deterministic, in the sense that the same input when provided to the same hash function produces the same hash value every time.
4. It is infeasible (though not impossible!) to invert and generate the message from its hash value, except trying for all possible messages.
5. Any small change in the message should greatly influence the output hash, just so no one can correlate the new hash value with the old one after a small change.

=== Slide 34 ===
DIGITAL SIGNATURE (MORE IN-DEPTH)
Digital signatures are made by combining asymmetric algorithms and message digests.
Recap: in asymmetric cryptography:
- If the private, secret key is used for encryption:
  -> the public key will bring back the original message.
- If something decrypts successfully with a public key:
  -> the only one who could have encrypted the message would be the one owning the matching private key.
  -> It means a specific person encrypted (signed) it.
  -> It means we can verify who encrypted the message.

=== Slide 35 ===
SIGN A MESSAGE
1. Run the message through a cryptographic hash function to make a fingerprint.
2. Encrypts the fingerprint using private key.
3. Pass the encrypted fingerprint along with the message.

=== Slide 36 ===
VERIFY A SIGNATURE
1. Pick up the public key of the sender and use the key for decrypting the fingerprint.
2. Run the message through the same cryptographic hash function.
3. Compare the locally generated fingerprint with the fingerprint he just decrypted.
4. If a match is found, then the message was unaltered, and that it was generated by the person owning the private key.

=== Slide 37 ===
PUBLIC KEY CERTIFICATES
While we no longer need a separate channel for the key in asymmetric cryptography, we still need a way to establish trust. Why?
- When encrypting, how do we know that we have the public key of the recipient, and not of some man in the middle?

Problem:
- When verifying a digital signature, how do we know that the public key we use is from the legitimate sender and not of someone faking both the message and the key?

=== Slide 38 ===
Solution:
- Trusted third-party, an entity trusted by the one who wants to make sure he has the correct public key.

A public key certificate is simply a data structure containing, among other things, the public key of the certificate owner.
- The entire structure is digitally signed by the trusted third-party, which most often is the entity who made the certificate; the CA (Certification Authority).

Using the public keys that we trust (the CA keys) we may verify the trustworthiness of other keys.

=== Slide 39 ===
Another problem: The public key of the certificate owner
We need to get the public key of the Certification Authority in a way that has some off-line trust establishment.
- In a web client setting, for instance, the CA keys come with the browser.

=== Slide 41 ===
PASSWORD-BASED AUTHENTICATION
Using Username and Password as credentials is the most common method for authentication.
Question: What is the biggest problem with passwords?
- passwords are static:
  - the same credential is used for every log-in.

Multi-factor authentication
- Password-base authentication can be combined with a possessed item, such as:
  - a list of one-time codes
  - a file containing a private key
  - a device for calculating access codes

=== Slide 42 ===
HOW IMPORTANT IS THE PROTECTION OF OUR USERS' PASSWORDS
Questions:
- How many sites, servers or systems do you log-in to regularly?
- On how many sites, servers or systems have you registered with a username and password?
- How many different passwords do you use?

The users tend to reuse some passwords for multiple websites.
- If a hacker cracks into the database and gets hold of all the passwords.
- The user trusts us to keep their multi-kingdom key.
- As a web application developer, even if we create a low-security site, we should not be careless about the passwords.

=== Slide 43 ===
HOW TO STORE PASSWORDS?
Question: Do we need to store passwords in our web application database?
Answer: NO!

=== Slide 44 ===
Never store clear-text passwords

So how to authenticate a user?
- We pass the password through a one-way hash function and store the fingerprint of the password in our database.
- Whenever the user wants to log-in, we take the user-provided clear-text password, pass it through the same hashing function, and compare the result with whatever we have in our database.

=== Slide 45 ===
PASSWORD SALTING
Salting is used to make sure two hashed passwords are different even if the passwords they represent are the same.
Why?
- Nobody can recognize a password, if have access to the hashed passwords.

A common strategy:
- is to combine the username and password into a new string and pass that string through the hash function.
- What is an ideal strategy? -> use a random salt per user, store salt + hash together.

=== Slide 46 ===
CRACKING PASSWORDS
Whether a password is hashed or plain text, an attacker will most likely need a computer to "guess", by rapidly testing a large number of candidates.
It will have to use a systematic approach to select candidates.
Two common approaches exist:
- Brute-force attack (exhaustive search/generate and test strategy)
- Dictionary-based attack

=== Slide 47 ===
BRUTE FORCE ATTACK
The term brute force is generally used to describe something in which every possible value is tested in turn.
- with brute-force password attacks, the program will create passwords of all possible lengths using permutations of valid password characters.
- The total number of combinations can be prohibitively large.

Example:
Password length could be between 4 to 10 characters
- 52 upper- and lower-case letters
- 10 digits
- 40 other characters
= 102 possible characters

102^4 + 102^5 + 102^6 + ... + 102^10 = 123,106,367,167,786,282,032 possible passwords

=== Slide 48 ===
- Programming mistakes may ease the job for brute force attack.
- Example: Windows NT Lan Manager (LM) passwords are converted to upper case, padded to 14 characters, and then split in two before being passed to the hash function as two separate, seven-character passwords.
  Brute force attacks against this kind of password are highly successful with today's computers.
- Brute-force may be the only option for totally random passwords, but for human-generated passwords, attacks based on dictionaries may give results much faster, even for passwords longer than eight characters.

=== Slide 49 ===
DICTIONARY ATTACK
Many passwords (human generated) are based on words in a dictionary.
- Modern password crackers use dictionaries, and they use a large set of rules to decide how to manipulate the words.
- The rules may dictate the appending of all sorts of characters, putting new characters in between existing characters, reversing words, replacing characters with numbers that look the same, etc.

Dictionary-based attacks work best when the goal is to crack one or more human passwords from a long list of password hashes.
- It may not work that well when the target is one particular password, and that password appears to be strong.

=== Slide 50 ===
ONLINE PASSWORD CRACKING
Online password cracking is more challenging.
- The network and web server overhead will make this process thousands of times slower.
- A huge number of failed log-in attempts in a short time raise suspicion of any system administrator.
- Many web applications restrict the number of failed log-in attempts for a user, and block further log-in attempts after this number is reached.

Web applications should have methods for discovering this kind of attack.
- Note that blocking log-in after a number of failed attempts may open up for Denial of Service (DOS) attacks, in which one user deliberately makes it impossible for another user to log in.

=== Slide 51 ===
REMEMBER ME
This functionality intrinsically may pose a security risk.
An attacker may use remember me to access the victim's data.
- Any person with access to the same computer may appear as the legitimate user without knowing the password.

It would be more dangerous if a web application implements the "remember me" functionality in an unhealthy way.

=== Slide 52 ===
Some sites store username/password in a cookie.
- When the cookie reaches the web site, the server-side code may authenticate the user based on the cookie.

Problem:
- Increases the number of times the credentials pass the network. It increases the risk of password leakage, particularly if we do not use SSL/TLS.
- Persistent cookies are stored in a file on the client computer.
- Many popular web browsers have had serious vulnerabilities that make it possible for someone on the outside to request all stored cookies using Cross-site Scripting.

=== Slide 53 ===
Secret Identifiers

=== Slide 54 ===
SECRET IDENTIFIER
Session ID (Recap):
- Instead of passwords, an ID is stored in a cookie in the user's browser, and a copy on the server as well.
- The user is identified using the ID sent in a cookie.
- We may allow the user to log in from several computers.
- We may use the same ID or use separate IDs for every computer.

The ID should not be anything an attacker may use directly (a password) or indirectly (a hashed password that may be cracked) on other web sites visited by our users.
- Secret IDs is not only for "remember me" functionality, but any ID for any resource on the server-side must be considered as secret ID.

=== Slide 55 ===
CAN THE SECRET IDENTIFIER BE GUESSED?
A secret ID should not be guessable.
- Not guessable means not related to anything.
  - Not related to anything probably means random.

Examples of weak ID of the server side resources:
- The URL of the last year's earnings report, and the report of the current year are different in year value only.
- All articles in the database of a library are numbered successively.

=== Slide 56 ===
Summary

=== Slide 57 ===
SUMMARY
- Encryption may be symmetric or asymmetric, depending on whether the same or two different keys are used for encryption and decryption.
  - When one key is used for both, one needs a secure channel to communicate the key.
  - When different keys are used, no separate channel is needed.
- A message may be signed by passing it through a cryptographic hash function that returns a message digest (fingerprint) and then have that fingerprint encrypted with the sender's private key.

=== Slide 58 ===
SUMMARY (continued)
- Anyone in possession of the matching public key may verify the signature.
- The public key may be distributed as part of a digital certificate, a data structure that is signed by a trusted third-party, such as a Certification Authority (CA).
- We must store hashed passwords rather than clear-text passwords.
  - With hashed passwords, people find it extremely difficult to get access to our users' passwords even if they successfully break into our site.

=== Slide 59 ===
SUMMARY (continued)
- Programs exist for doing both brute-force and dictionary-based attacks.
  - Poor passwords, which unfortunately are quite common, may be found in a very short time.
- Secret IDs should be used to stop people from getting access to restricted, server-side resources.
- The sessions should not be available to anyone but the owner.
- Secret IDs should be made from long, truly random numbers.
  - Traditional pseudo-random number generators do not provide numbers that are good enough.
